Are Gift Cards Still a Good Gift in 2025?

Here's What the Data Actually Says

The gift card debate has raged for decades. Are they thoughtful gifts that give recipients exactly what they want? Or are they lazy cop-outs that scream "I forgot to shop until the last minute"?

Let's cut through the opinions and look at what the data actually tells us. After analyzing multiple consumer surveys, market research studies, and billions of dollars in gift card transactions, the answer is more nuanced—and more interesting—than you might think.

90% of Americans consider physical gift cards an appropriate item to gift, according to Capital One Shopping research.

The Gift Card Market: Bigger Than You Think

Gift cards aren't just popular—they're massive. The U.S. gift card market was valued at $448 billion in 2024 and is projected to grow 11.4% annually. Globally, the market hit $1.24 trillion in 2025 and is expected to reach $2.31 trillion by 2030.

Gift cards consistently rank as the most-requested gift item, second only to clothing during the holidays. Nearly half of Americans plan to give gift cards each holiday season, making them more popular than electronics, toys, or home goods.

Bottom line: When 90% of people say gift cards are appropriate and they're the #2 most-requested gift, the data suggests they're not just acceptable—they're actually what people want.

The Case FOR Gift Cards (Backed by Data)

✓ THE PROS

  • Recipients actually want them: 84% of consumers enjoy receiving gift cards
  • They drive more spending: 61% of recipients spend an average of $31.75 MORE than the card's value
  • Convenience reigns: 52% of consumers buy gift cards specifically because they're convenient
  • They introduce new businesses: 57% said receiving a gift card would prompt them to try a new restaurant or store they otherwise wouldn't visit
  • No wrong size or color: Zero returns, exchanges, or disappointed faces
  • Quick redemption: 76% of consumers use their gift card entirely within one month of receiving it

✗ THE CONS

  • Massive waste problem: 47% of Americans have unused gift cards worth an average of $187 each—that's $23 billion in unspent money nationwide
  • They get lost: 17% of people have lost at least one gift card in their lifetime
  • Expiration issues: 20% have had at least one gift certificate expire before redeeming
  • Can feel impersonal: Critics argue they lack the thoughtfulness of a carefully chosen gift
  • Stores close: 12% have had a business close permanently before using their gift card
  • People hoard them: 71% have held onto a gift card for at least one year

What Makes Gift Cards Work (or Fail)

The research reveals that gift cards work best when they're given thoughtfully. Here's what separates a great gift card from a forgettable one:

1. Match the Card to the Person

Generic Visa gift cards are practical but impersonal. The magic happens when you choose a retailer that aligns with someone's specific interests. A Sephora card for your makeup-loving sister shows you know her. An REI card for your outdoorsy friend proves you pay attention.

According to TSG and Bank of America's 2025 survey of over 1,000 consumers, 63% of gift card buyers prefer designs that align with the special occasion, as opposed to store-branded designs. Presentation matters.

3. Digital vs. Physical: The New Debate

The gift card world is splitting into two camps, and your choice matters more than you think.

Physical Gift Cards:

  • 54% of consumers still prefer them
  • 97% of Baby Boomers use physical cards
  • Better for in-person gifting occasions
  • Tangible—something to unwrap

Digital Gift Cards:

  • Growing 26% annually (vs. 9% for physical)
  • 75% of Millennials have used them
  • 77% appreciate immediate delivery
  • Perfect for last-minute gifts
  • Impossible to physically lose
  • Eco-friendly option
Interesting trend: 48% of consumers have bought more digital gift cards than physical ones over the last two years, showing a major shift in behavior driven by the pandemic and increased online shopping.

The $23 Billion Problem

Here's where gift cards get controversial: Americans collectively hold $23 billion in unused gift card value. That's not a typo—$23 billion sitting in wallets, forgotten in drawers, or expired in the trash.

Why does this happen?

  • Forgetfulness: Out of sight, out of mind
  • Procrastination: 71% of people hold onto gift cards for at least a year, waiting for the "right" purchase
  • Small balances: After partial use, $3.47 left on a card isn't worth a trip
  • Location issues: Store not nearby or not available online
  • Disinterest: Gift card to a store they don't like

This "breakage" is actually a massive profit center for retailers. When you don't use that $50 Cheesecake Factory card, they keep the money at 100% profit margin—no food costs, no labor, nothing.

The Psychology Behind Gift Cards

Here's what's fascinating: gift cards occupy a unique psychological space. They're not quite cash (too impersonal) and not quite a gift (no thought required). They're somewhere in between—and that's actually their strength.

Why gift cards work psychologically:

  • "Found money" effect: Recipients treat gift card spending differently than their own money, often splurging on items they'd normally skip
  • Permission to indulge: A spa gift card gives license to pamper yourself guilt-free
  • Social proof: When a trusted friend gives you a gift card, it's an implicit recommendation of that business
  • Choice preservation: Recipients get exactly what they want without the giver having to guess

When Gift Cards Are the WRONG Choice

Let's be honest: gift cards aren't always appropriate. Here's when you should skip them:

  • Close romantic relationships: Your spouse/partner deserves more thought and personalization
  • Young children: Kids want toys they can open and play with, not plastic cards
  • When you know exactly what they want: If they've shown you the specific item, buy the actual thing
  • Milestone celebrations: Major birthdays, weddings, and graduations often warrant more meaningful gifts
  • When the store doesn't match their taste: A gift card to a store they never shop at is worse than cash

How to Make Gift Cards More Personal

If you're giving a gift card, here's how to elevate it beyond "lazy gift" territory:

  1. Choose themed designs: 63% of buyers prefer occasion-specific designs—Christmas themes, birthday graphics, or seasonal art
  2. Pair with a small physical gift: A coffee shop gift card in a nice mug, or a bookstore card with a bookmark
  3. Write a heartfelt note: Explain why you chose that specific store and what you hope they'll get
  4. Create a scavenger hunt: Make the reveal an experience, especially for kids or close friends
  5. Package creatively: Present it in an origami envelope, a handmade holder, or a decorative box

The Verdict: Are Gift Cards Good Gifts?

After analyzing all the data, here's the truth: gift cards are good gifts when given thoughtfully, but terrible gifts when given thoughtlessly.

The difference comes down to three factors:

  1. Match: Does the retailer align with their interests and tastes?
  2. Relationship: Is this appropriate for your level of closeness?
  3. Presentation: Did you put effort into making it special?

The data is clear: 90% of people find them appropriate, 84% enjoy receiving them, and they consistently rank as a top-requested gift. But those same surveys show billions in waste and concerns about thoughtfulness.

Final answer: Gift cards are excellent gifts for most relationships when chosen with care and presented with thought. They become problematic only when used as a universal substitute for all gift-giving with zero personalization.

Smart Gift Card Strategies for 2025

For Gift Givers:

  • Buy digital gift cards for immediate delivery—78% send them instantly
  • Choose retailers specific to their hobbies (not generic department stores)
  • Consider the average value: most people expect $25-50 for casual relationships, $50+ for close ones
  • Screenshot or print the confirmation and present it creatively
  • Send a reminder text 2-3 weeks later: "Don't forget about your gift card!"

For Gift Recipients:

  • Add to your phone's digital wallet so you don't lose them

Frequently Asked Questions

Are gift cards tacky or cheap?

Data says no: 90% of Americans consider them appropriate gifts. They only feel tacky when given with zero thought to the recipient's interests or when used universally for all relationships without personalization.

How much should I spend on a gift card?

The average gift card value is $47.91. Most people expect $25-50 for colleagues/acquaintances, $50-100 for friends/family, and $100+ for very close relationships or major occasions.

Do people actually use gift cards?

Yes! 76% use them entirely within one month. However, 47% of Americans have at least one unused card worth an average of $187.

Are digital gift cards better than physical?

Depends on the situation. Digital cards are growing faster (26% annually), offer immediate delivery, and can't be physically lost. Physical cards feel more tangible and work better for in-person gifting. 54% still prefer physical, but 48% have recently bought more digital cards.

What if they don't like the store I chose?

This is why store selection matters. Choose retailers you KNOW they shop at, or opt for open-loop Visa gift cards that work anywhere. About 24% of people regift cards they won't use, and 9% resell them on secondary markets.

The Bottom Line

Gift cards get a bad rap from people who claim they're impersonal, but the data tells a different story. When 90% of people say they're appropriate, 84% enjoy receiving them, and they're consistently the #2 most-requested gift, the numbers don't lie.

The key is thoughtfulness. A carefully chosen gift card to someone's favorite restaurant, paired with a heartfelt note and creative presentation, is miles ahead of a generic sweater in the wrong size.

Gift cards aren't the problem—thoughtless gift-giving is. And that can happen with any gift, whether it's wrapped in fancy paper or loaded onto a plastic card.
